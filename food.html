Foods & Drinks

Discover the rich and flavorful world of South Korean cuisine, where every dish is crafted with balance, tradition, and passion. From sizzling Korean barbecue and colorful bibimbap bowls to the iconic taste of kimchi, Korean food offers a perfect harmony of spicy, sweet, and savory flavors. Whether you’re exploring comforting home-cooked meals or mouthwatering street snacks like tteokbokki and hotteok, each bite tells a story of culture and community. Dive in and experience why South Korean cuisine continues to capture hearts — and appetites — around the world.

Eating ettiquette

But before diving into the delicious world of South Korean cuisine, it’s important to understand the dining etiquette that accompanies it. Korean meals are not just about the food — they’re about respect, harmony, and shared experience. Learning these customs helps you appreciate the food even more, allowing you to enjoy each dish the way it’s meant to be — with mindfulness and respect.

In Korea, dining manners are taken seriously.

  • You should wait for the eldest to start eating before you do
  • never lift your bowl while eating
  • avoid sticking chopsticks upright in rice (it resembles funeral rituals)
  • It’s also polite to pour drinks for others rather than yourself.

Meals are usually shared. A group eats from the same set of dishes rather than having individual portions. This reflects the importance of harmony and togetherness in Korean culture. Side Dishes (Banchan) are small plates served with rice, usually including kimchi, pickled vegetables, seasoned greens, fish cakes, or pancakes. Banchan is unlimited in many restaurants and represents balance and variety in Korean meals.


Traditional Dishes

Japchae (잡채) — Glass noodles stir-fried with vegetables and light soy seasoning.

Kimchi-jjigae (김치찌개) — Hearty kimchi stew with tofu and pork.

Samgyetang (삼계탕) — Ginseng chicken soup for health and energy.

Nakji-bokkeum (낙지볶음) - Stir-fried octopus with vegetables, often spicy (gochujang, chili flakes etc.), served over rice. Though relatively more recent (mid-20th century), it has become a popular “spicy seafood” dish

Gukbap (국밥) — Soup served with rice, a comforting Korean staple.

Drinks

Soju — Korea’s famous clear spirit, smooth and slightly sweet.

Makgeolli — Traditional milky rice wine with a tangy sweetness.

Tea — Traditional teas like green tea (nokcha) and barley tea (boricha) are popular and historic.
